# Portfolio Reflection

## Understanding User Needs

Working through the Product Owner role changed the way I think about
developing software. My first instinct used to be thinking about how I would
build a feature. I realized that understanding what the customer actually
wants should come before thinking about the technical solution. Creating user
stories helped me focus on the user's perspective, organize the product
backlog, and prioritize the features that would provide the most value. It
also made it easier to see why Agile puts so much emphasis on customer
feedback, since priorities can change throughout development.

## My Development Process

Before taking this course, I mostly thought of software development as writing
code. I can see now that development starts long before any code is written.
Planning, gathering requirements, communicating with stakeholders, testing,
and reviewing progress all play an important role in building successful
software.

The Agile practice I see myself using the most is breaking larger projects
into smaller, more manageable pieces. It makes projects feel less
overwhelming, gives me a chance to review my progress more often, and makes it
easier to adjust when requirements change. Instead of waiting until the end
of a project to discover problems, I'd rather catch them early and make small
improvements along the way.

## Being a Good Team Member

Working through each Scrum role also showed me that being a good team member
isn't just about writing good code. Every role depended on the others, and it
became clear that communication is what keeps a project moving forward.
Whether someone is gathering requirements, writing code, testing the
application, or helping remove roadblocks, everyone contributes to the final
product.

I also realized that being a good teammate means being willing to ask
questions, share progress, and accept feedback from others. Software projects
change constantly, and no one has every answer. Being open to different ideas
and communicating early helps prevent small problems from turning into much
bigger ones later.

By the end of this course, I had a much better understanding of what software
development actually looks like outside of writing code. I'll continue using
these ideas throughout the rest of my degree, in personal projects, and as I
work toward a career in game development.
